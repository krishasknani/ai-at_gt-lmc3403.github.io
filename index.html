The Problem

"Working hard and struggling is actually an important way of learning. When you're given an answer, you're not struggling and you're not learning."

- Dr. Eric Klopfer, MIT

What is "Vibe Coding"?

Vibe coding is a term coined by Andrej Karpathy to describe a new approach to programming where developers rely heavily on AI tools like ChatGPT, GitHub Copilot, or Claude to generate code based on natural language descriptions. Rather than understanding the underlying logic, programmers describe what they want and let AI handle the implementation.

While this can boost productivity for experienced developers, it poses significant risks for students who skip the fundamental learning process entirely.

AI in the Tech Industry

The impact on the tech industry has been massive, with thousands losing their jobs at companies like Microsoft and Accenture. Looking into the future, executives in the industry are hoping to replace even more coding jobs. Mark Zuckerberg, the CEO of Meta, suggests that this could happen within the next year as mid-level engineer positions could be replaced with AI.

This indicates that the current trend in the tech space is to embrace LLMs and other artificial intelligence technology, and that jobs are more strategic in nature as more of the coding workload is replaced with AI. As Georgia Tech prepares its computer science students for their future careers, some CS professors might want to mirror this trend in their curriculum and assignments.

Why is this important to the student body?

The future of LLMs and AI in computer science education is uncertain. Excess usage can lead to vibe coding and poor learning, but the industry is pushing for more AI tools. This means that if Georgia Tech wants to effectively prepare their students for the future, it may be in their best interest to support AI tools. Our early research has shown that developing assignments with novelty, rigidity, and complexity leads to assignments that have challenges that cannot be circumvented by copy and pasting from a chatbot. In order to properly adapt and implement helpful guidelines, the College of Computing needs to hear from students to understand the current situation on AI use to create the best guidelines for successful education.

Our Solution

A Framework for AI Integration, informed by GT Students

Student Body Survey

We're conducting a student body-wide survey to understand how Georgia Tech students currently use AI in their coursework.

Assignment Classification Framework

Novelty

How new or unfamiliar is the material? Higher novelty assignments should restrict AI usage to ensure students grapple with new concepts firsthand.

Rigidity

How fixed are the requirements? Highly rigid assignments with specific correct answers may benefit from AI restrictions, while open-ended projects could allow more flexibility.

Complexity

What is the cognitive load required? Complex, multi-step problems should be protected learning opportunities where students develop systematic thinking skills.

Process-Oriented Grading

Traditional outcome-based grading incentivizes students to get the "right answer" by any means necessary. Our proposed process-oriented grading approach shifts focus to:

Process-oriented grading also encourages deliberate reflection. By asking students to document their decisions and how they debugged code, instructors gain clearer insight into a student's actual understanding. This shifts grading away from "Did it run?" toward "How did you get there?" which is a far more reliable measure of learning.

Why are we conducting this survey?

Georgia Tech's Academic Honor Code currently does not mention AI. This leaves students and professors without clear guidance on what constitutes appropriate use of tools like ChatGPT, GitHub Copilot, and other AI assistants in coursework.

We want to understand where you stand on the spectrum of AI usage‚Äîfrom writing all your code from scratch to fully automating tasks. Your input will directly inform the development of clear, fair guidelines for the College of Computing.
